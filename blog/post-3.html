What to Look For When Hiring a Pool Contractor

January 20, 2026 [Author Name] 6 min read

A new pool is a $40,000–$100,000+ investment depending on size, features, and finish. The contractor you hire determines whether that investment produces a backyard oasis your family enjoys for 25 years — or a costly dispute over an unfinished shell. Most homeowners spend more time researching a refrigerator than they do vetting a pool contractor. This guide changes that.

The pool construction industry has relatively low barriers to entry. Anyone with a truck, a few subcontractor relationships, and a state contractor's license can start taking deposits. The wide quality spectrum between operators makes due diligence essential. Here's what to look for at every stage.

Verify Licensing and Insurance Before the First Meeting

Every legitimate pool contractor operating in a regulated state carries a valid contractor's license specific to pool or swimming pool construction. This is not optional — it's a legal requirement in most jurisdictions. A contractor who hesitates when you ask for their license number is an immediate red flag.

Verify the license directly with your state contractor licensing board — don't just accept a number written on a business card. State licensing databases are publicly searchable online. Look for: active status (not expired or suspended), no disciplinary actions on record, and that the license classification matches the work (pool/spa, not just general contractor).

Insurance has two components you need to confirm. General liability insurance protects your property if something goes wrong during construction. Workers' compensation insurance protects you from liability if a worker is injured on your property. Ask for a Certificate of Insurance — a document your insurance agent can review — not just verbal confirmation. The COI should list your name and address as an additional insured.

"The most common story I hear goes like this: a homeowner hired a contractor who was $20,000 cheaper than everyone else. Six months later the contractor was unreachable, the pool shell had problems, and nobody on the crew had workers' comp. The homeowner ended up paying more to fix it than a quality contractor would have charged in the first place."

Check Their Local Track Record — Not Just Star Ratings

Online reviews give you signal, but the information that matters most is harder to find. Ask every contractor you're considering for three to five recent local references — homeowners whose pools were completed in the past 12–24 months. Then actually call them.

When you speak with references, ask specific questions: Did the project finish on schedule? How did the contractor handle surprises or change orders? Was the final cost close to the original estimate? Were there any post-completion issues, and how were they handled? How responsive was the contractor after the job was done?

If a contractor can't produce local references, that's a problem. If they produce references but the homeowners seem rehearsed or won't give direct answers, trust your instincts. A contractor who does great work wants you to talk to their past customers.

Also check the Better Business Bureau, your county courthouse records for liens or judgments, and the state contractor board's complaint history. A single complaint is not necessarily disqualifying — how a company resolves complaints matters. Multiple unresolved complaints are a dealbreaker.

What to Look For in the Contract and Estimate

A professional pool contractor provides a detailed written contract before any work begins or any deposit is collected. Be skeptical of vague one-page proposals. A proper contract should clearly specify:

  • Exact scope of work: pool dimensions, shape, depth, finish material (plaster type, color), equipment brands and model numbers for pump, filter, heater, and sanitizer system.
  • Payment schedule tied to milestones: never a large upfront payment not tied to specific completed work. Typical structure: deposit at signing, payment at excavation, payment at shell completion, payment at equipment installation, final payment at completion.
  • Timeline with start and estimated completion dates, and language about what happens if the timeline is missed.
  • Change order process: any scope change must be in writing, signed by both parties, with cost and timeline impact documented before the work is done.
  • Warranty terms: what is covered, for how long, and who handles warranty claims — the contractor or the manufacturer directly.
  • Permit responsibility: the contractor should pull all required permits and pass all inspections before covering work. Never accept a contractor who suggests skipping permits.

Questions Every Homeowner Should Ask Before Signing

Beyond licensing and references, ask these questions directly. Pay attention not just to the answers, but to how the contractor responds — a confident, experienced professional will answer without hesitation.

Who physically builds the pool? Many contractors are primarily salespeople who subcontract the entire construction process. This isn't always bad, but you want to know who is on your property and who is accountable if something goes wrong. Get the names of subcontractors and verify their credentials too.

What happens if you go over budget? Cost overruns are more common in pool construction than almost any other home improvement project — soil conditions, utility conflicts, permit requirements, and material price changes all affect the final number. Ask how they handle surprises and get the answer in writing in the contract.

How do you handle the permit and inspection process? An experienced contractor navigates this routinely and should be able to explain it clearly. If they're vague, they may not be pulling permits at all — a serious legal and financial risk for you as the property owner.

The right contractor won't be threatened by these questions. They'll welcome them — because good contractors know that an informed homeowner becomes a satisfied customer, and satisfied customers are their best source of future work.
